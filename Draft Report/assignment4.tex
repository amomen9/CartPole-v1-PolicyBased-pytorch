\begin{abstract}
This report studies Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO), a state-of-the-art on-policy actor-critic algorithm, applied to the CartPole environment. We motivate and derive the \textbf{clipped surrogate policy} loss and the value loss, and we relate them to the vanilla policy gradient used in \textbf{REINFORCE}, \textbf{AC} and \textbf{A2C}. We implement the \textbf{PPO-clipped algorithm} strengthened with \textbf{Generalised Advantage Estimation (GAE)}, as well, as our advantage estimator. Then the resulting learning curves are compared against the baseline given in Assignment 2, DQN, AC and A2C and we observe how PPO with GAE performs trying to attain the CartPole optimum  across repetitions of the studied methods.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

In previous assignments we covered the main building blocks of deep reinforcement learning: a \textbf{pure value-based method} (\textbf{DQN}), a \textbf{pure policy-gradient method} (\textbf{REINFORCE}) and two \textbf{hybrid actor-critic methods} (\textbf{AC} and \textbf{A2C}). While these algorithms cleanly illustrate the trade-off between bias and variance in policy-gradient estimation, none of them is state-of-the-art. In particular, vanilla policy-gradient updates suffer from two practical issues: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item each interaction with the environment is used for exactly one gradient step, which is \textbf{sample-inefficient}, 
    \item Unbounded importance weights produced by larger step sizes destabilise training. 
\end{enumerate}
\textbf{Proximal Policy Optimisation} (PPO) addresses both issues by reusing each batch of on-policy data for several gradient epochs (by Sutton \& Barto's definition of off-policy, running off-policy for K batches, but according to the common definition it is on-policy, because it reuses still fresh samples and is not similar to \textbf{Experience Replay} we had before which uses arbitrarily old samples) and by \textbf{clipping} the \textbf{importance sampling ratio} so that the new policy never strays too far from the policy that collected the data.
In addition to the base PPO algorithm, we also have used \textbf{Generalised Advantage Estimation (GAE)} \cite{schulman2016gae} extension for the \textbf{advantage estimator}. There are other extensions such as adding entropy but are not studied in this report. \autoref{sec:theory} introduces the relevant equations, derives the clipped objective from the policy-gradient objective used in A2C, and presents the algorithm pseudocode. \autoref{sec:experiments} describes the environment and experimental design. The learning curves are reported in \autoref{sec:results} and analysed in \autoref{sec:discussion}. We conclude in \autoref{sec:conclusion}.

\section{Theory}\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{From policy gradient to PPO}\label{sec:theory:pg}

A stochastic policy $\pi_\theta(a|s)$ assigns a probability to each action $a$ in state $s$ given parameters $\theta\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$. The objective is the expected discounted return $J(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{H-1}\gamma^t r_t\right]$ where $\gamma\in[0,1]$ is the discount factor and $\tau=\{s_0,a_0,r_0,\dots,s_N\}$ is a trajectory of $N$ sampled from $\pi_\theta$. The policy-gradient theorem expresses its gradient as
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{N-1} \hat A_t \nabla_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)\right],
    \label{eq:pg}
\end{equation}
where $\hat A_t$ is an advantage estimator of action $a_t$ in state $s_t$. REINFORCE uses the Monte-Carlo return $\hat A_t=\hat Q(s_t,a_t)$; AC additionally subtracts a baseline; A2C uses the $n$-step advantage $\hat A_n(s_t,a_t)=\hat Q_n(s_t,a_t)-V_\varphi(s_t)$ where $V_\varphi$ is the critic estimated by parameters $\varphi$.

PPO retains \cref{eq:pg} but does \emph{not} apply the resulting update in a single gradient step. Instead, a batch of on-policy transitions is reused for $K$ epochs (rewarding us with more sampling efficiency). Because the policy changes between epochs, the data are off-policy with respect to $\pi_\theta$. PPO compensates with an importance \textbf{ratio}
\begin{equation}
    r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a_t|s_t)},
    \label{eq:ratio}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{\mathrm{old}}$ are the parameters at the start of the update. Replacing $\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)$ by $\nabla_\theta r_t(\theta)$ gives the linearised surrogate (importance sampling correction)
\begin{equation}
    L^{\mathrm{CPI}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[r_t(\theta)\hat A_t\right].
    \label{eq:cpi}
\end{equation}
A large $r_t(\theta)$ together with a positive $\hat A_t$ can cause arbitrarily large policy updates and thus instability. PPO replaces $L^{\mathrm{CPI}}$ by the pessimistic clipped surrogate
\begin{equation}
    \!L^{\mathrm{CLIP}}\!(\theta)\! =\! \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\min\!\big(r_t(\theta)\hat A_t,\,\mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta),1\!-\!\epsilon,1\!+\!\epsilon)\hat A_t\big)\right]\!\!,
    \label{eq:clip}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon\in(0,1)$ is the clipping range. The $\min$ ensures that the gradient vanishes (is clipped) once the ratio leaves $[1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]$ in the direction that would otherwise improve the surrogate, so the new policy is bound to stay within a small neighbourhood of $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}$. The actor parameters are updated by gradient ascent (or descent) on \cref{eq:clip}.

The expectations in \cref{eq:cpi}--\cref{eq:clip} are estimated from a fixed-length \emph{rollout buffer} of $T$ on-policy transitions $\{(s_t,a_t,r_t)\}_{t=0}^{T-1}$ collected by $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}$ before each update; the buffer may span several episodes when an episode is shorter than $T$, or be \textbf{truncated} mid-episode when it is longer. The rollout length $T$ (written in the code as rollout\_steps) is a hyperparameter and trades two opposing effects: larger $T$ lowers the variance of the empirical advantages and of the ratio statistics (bias favor), but it also lets the policy drift further from $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}$ over the $K$ epochs of updates, increasing the importance-sampling bias that the clipping in \cref{eq:clip} must absorb.

The number of epochs $K$ is what makes the clipping in \cref{eq:clip} effective: At $K=1$ a single gradient step keeps $r_t(\theta)$ inside $[1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]$, leaving the $\min$ and $\mathrm{clip}$ in \cref{eq:clip} as ineffective, so the surrogate is in expectation \cref{eq:cpi} and PPO degenerates to A2C-with-GAE on a fixed rollout. Choosing $K>1$ therefore trades sample efficiency against the divergence between $\pi_\theta$ and $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}$: each additional epoch extracts more gradient signal from the same $T$ transitions, but it also pushes more samples to the clip boundary where their gradient vanishes, so for sufficiently large $K$ the policy stops improving even though gradient steps are still being taken.

The critic $V_\varphi$ is trained to predict the bootstrapped return $\hat R_t = \hat A_t + V_\varphi(s_t)$ used inside the advantage. The value loss is
\begin{equation}
    L^{V}(\varphi) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\big(V_\varphi(s_t) - \hat R_t\big)^{2}\right].
    \label{eq:vloss}
\end{equation}
Actor and critic are optimised independently with learning rates $\alpha$ and $\beta$ (actor\_lr critic\_lr in the code).

\subsection{Generalised Advantage Estimation}\label{sec:theory:gae}

A2C estimates the advantage at depth $n$ as $\hat A_n(s_t,a_t)=\hat Q_n(s_t,a_t)-V_\varphi(s_t)$. The choice of $n$ trades \textbf{bias} against \textbf{variance}: small $n$ is low-variance (like \textbf{TD(0)} that we had) but \textbf{high-bias}, large $n$ is the \textbf{converse}. GAE \cite{schulman2016gae} interpolates between all depths with a single hyperparameter $\lambda\in[0,1]$. With the TD-residual $\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_\varphi(s_{t+1}) - V_\varphi(s_t)$, the GAE advantage is the exponentially-weighted sum (geometric sum)
{\setlength{\abovedisplayskip}{6pt}%
\setlength{\belowdisplayskip}{15pt}%
\setlength{\abovedisplayshortskip}{6pt}%
\setlength{\belowdisplayshortskip}{15pt}%
\begin{equation}
\hat A_t^{\mathrm{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^{l}\,\delta_{t+l}.
\label{eq:gae}
\end{equation}}
$\lambda=0$ reduces to the one-step TD(0) advantage; $\lambda=1$ reduces to the Monte-Carlo advantage as used in AC. We use this estimator as $\hat A_t$ in \cref{eq:clip} and the corresponding return $\hat R_t$ in \cref{eq:vloss}.

\subsection{Algorithm and relation to AC/A2C}\label{sec:theory:alg}

\autoref{alg:PPO} collects the components above. Compared to A2C (\autoref{alg:A2C-ref}), the structural differences are (i) PPO collects a fixed rollout of $T$ steps that may span several episodes before updating, whereas A2C updates per episode (or per $n$-step) \textbf{once}; (ii) PPO uses GAE (in my report) \cref{eq:gae} rather than an $n$-step advantage; (iii) PPO performs $K$ full-batch gradient passes over the same rollout, replacing the unbounded log-probability gradient by the clipped ratio surrogate \cref{eq:clip}. These three changes turn the on-policy actor-critic update into a sample-efficient, stable update at modest implementation cost.

\begingroup
\setlength{\intextsep}{30pt}
\setlength{\textfloatsep}{0pt}
\begin{algorithm}[H]
    \caption{PPO-clipped with GAE}
    \begin{algorithmic}
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} policy $\pi_\theta$ (actor), value $V_\varphi$ (critic), rollout length $T$, clip $\epsilon$, epochs $K$, GAE $\lambda$, discount $\gamma$, learning rates $\alpha,\beta$
        \STATE \textbf{Initialise:} $\theta,\varphi$ at random
        \WHILE{not converged}
            \STATE Collect rollout $\{(s_t,a_t,r_t,\log\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a_t|s_t),V_\varphi(s_t))\}_{t=0}^{T-1}$ with $\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}=\pi_\theta$
            \STATE Compute $\hat A_t^{\mathrm{GAE}}$ via \cref{eq:gae} and $\hat R_t=\hat A_t+V_\varphi(s_t)$
            \FOR{$k=1,\ldots,K$ epochs}
                \STATE $\theta\!\leftarrow\!\theta\!+\!\alpha\nabla_\theta L^{\mathrm{CLIP}}$, $\varphi\!\leftarrow\!\varphi\!-\!\beta\nabla_\varphi L^{V}$
            \ENDFOR
        \ENDWHILE
    \end{algorithmic}
    \label{alg:PPO}
\end{algorithm}
\endgroup

For comparison, the A2C update we used in Assignment 3 is reproduced below.
\begingroup
\setlength{\intextsep}{15pt}
\setlength{\textfloatsep}{0pt}
\begin{algorithm}[H]
    \caption{Advantage Actor Critic (A2C); reproduced from Assignment 3}
    \begin{algorithmic}
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} actor $\pi_\theta$, critic $V_\varphi$, episodes $M$, depth $n$, learning rates $\alpha,\beta$
        \STATE \textbf{Initialise:} $\theta,\varphi$ at random
        \FOR{$i=1,\ldots,M$}
            \STATE Sample trace $\tau=\{s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_T\}$
            \FOR{$t=0,\ldots,T-1$}
                \STATE $\hat Q_n(s_t,a_t)=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\gamma^k r_{t+k}+\gamma^{n}V_\varphi(s_{t+n})$
                \STATE $\hat A_n(s_t,a_t)=\hat Q_n(s_t,a_t)-V_\varphi(s_t)$
            \ENDFOR
            \STATE $\theta\!\leftarrow\!\theta+\alpha\sum_t \hat A_n(s_t,a_t)\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)$
            \STATE $\varphi\!\leftarrow\!\varphi-\beta\sum_t (\hat A_n(s_t,a_t))^{2}$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
    \label{alg:A2C-ref}
\end{algorithm}
\endgroup

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Environment:}\label{sec:experiments:env}

We use the CartPole-v1 environment again.

\subsection{Implementation and engineering tricks}\label{sec:experiments:impl}

We implement PPO-clipped following \autoref{alg:PPO}. The actor and critic are independent fully-connected networks. The implementation contains exactly one addition beyond the basic PPO policy-gradient method, and it is part of the algorithm \autoref{alg:PPO}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Generalised Advantage Estimation} (\cref{eq:gae}) is used to compute $\hat A_t$ from the rollout. Our implementation iterates backwards through the buffer with the standard recurrence $\hat A_t = \delta_t + \gamma\lambda\,(1-d_t)\,\hat A_{t+1}$, where $d_t$ is the\textbf{ terminal flag}, so that the bootstrap from $V_\varphi$ is \textbf{zeroed} at the end of an episode.
\end{itemize}

Each of the $K$ inner epochs is a single full-batch gradient step on the entire rollout.

\subsection{Experimental setup}\label{sec:experiments:setup}

Each configuration is trained for \textbf{$10^{6}$ environment steps} (n\_timesteps). \textbf{Returns} are recorded every \textbf{$250$ steps} (eval\_interval). Each learning curve is averaged over \textbf{5 (n\_repetitions)} independent seeds; we report the mean and a Student-$t$ confidence band around it for the curves. We have reduced the confidence interval for the curves to \textbf{80\% for a better and distinguishable visual demonstration} and for confidence intervals of the multiple curves to \textbf{less overlap}. Curves shown in the main text are smoothed with a suitable smoothing window. As in Assignments 2 and 3, \textbf{learning rate} and network widths were tuned first; the other HP were then varied around reasonable defaults. The optimal setting used in \autoref{fig:main_result} is:
\textbf{ $\alpha=3\!\times\!10^{-4}$, $\beta=10^{-2}$, $\gamma=0.99$, $\lambda=0.95$, $\epsilon=0.1$, rollout $T=512$, $K=30$} \textbf{epochs}, two hidden layers of \textbf{$128$ units} in the actor network and two \textbf{512-unit} hidden layers in the critic network. 
We choose $T$ as a power of 2 by convention and compliance, inherited from the original PPO implementation (by Schulman): my algorithm does not require it, but powers of \textbf{2} align rollout and mini-batch (that is not implemented in this report) tensors with the multiples of $32$ used by GPU tensor cores, which is the standard deep-learning batching convention.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Comparison with AC, A2C and DQN.}
\autoref{fig:main_result} shows that \textbf{PPO with GAE} reaches the optimum return of $500$ in fewer environment interactions and with a substantially narrower confidence band than \textbf{A2C} and \textbf{DQN} both. Two factors plausibly drive the variance improvement over A2C: \textbf{rollout reuse reduces gradient variance per environment step}, and the \textbf{clipped surrogate prevents the destructive large updates} that we observed as mild dips in the A2C curve in Assignment 3 before it converges. The behavior over DQN is consistent with what we already observed for the policy-gradient family in Assignment 3 - the on-policy character \textbf{better matches} the structure of CartPole's bounded, low-dimensional state space.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter sensitivity.}
The optimal hyperparameter configurations of all of the algorithms are on \textbf{Experiment.py} of the assignment.
During every hyperparameter sweeping trial, only the hyperparameter that is mentioned on the plot's legend has been sweeped for an optimal value and other hyperparameters have been kept \textbf{static} for better recognition.
The \textbf{learning rate} is by far the most important hyperparameter (\autoref{fig:hp_lr}). Consequently, although the sweep values are relatively close, the curve deviations are considerable. This is in line with the general intuition that learning rates dominate in RL. The GAE parameter $\lambda$ (\autoref{fig:hp_lambda}) shows the expected bias-variance pattern: $\lambda=1$ inherits the high variance of the Monte-Carlo advantage which has the highest curve instability, while $\lambda=0$ inherits the high bias of the one-step TD(0) advantage; values around $0.96$ are a good compromise on CartPole. The clipping range $\epsilon$ is far less sensitive, which mirrors the original PPO ablation.

\paragraph{Weaknesses and challenges.}
Our setup has several limitations. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Five seeds are sufficient to detect large effects but are too few to \textbf{resolve overlapping confidence intervals}, e.g.\ when comparing $\lambda=0.9$ and $\lambda=0.95$. \item with respect to the generalization of PPO algorithm's applications, another limitation is the one that CartPole environment inherently imposes itself. \textbf{CartPole is generally solved by very small networks}, just like our experiments in this course; conclusions about $\epsilon$ or $\lambda$ may not be transferable to higher-dimensional and \textbf{more sophisticated environments}. Other environments also might require different tuned hyperparameters.
    \item  By not having additional common \textbf{engineering tricks}, we cannot claim that our numbers represent the best achievable PPO performance on this domain.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We implemented\textbf{ PPO-clipped with GAE} and compared it on CartPole against the \textbf{baseline}, \textbf{DQN}, and \textbf{A2C}. The clipped surrogate and rollout reuse together explain the \textbf{empirical advantage} of PPO over A2C, while GAE controls the \textbf{bias-variance} trade-off without introducing additional hyperparameters. \textbf{Reflecting on the assignments}, we observed a \textbf{consistent progression}: value-only DQN, policy-only REINFORCE, advantage-based A2C, and finally PPO with GAE, each step trading additional algorithmic complexity for measurable empirical gains. 
\subsection{Future work}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Re-introducing the \textbf{engineering tricks} we have not employed (entropy bonus, value clipping, normalisation, gradient clipping) one at a time to quantify their individual contributions,
    \item Moving to a continuous-control and more complex benchmark where PPO's advantage over A2C is expected to be larger.
    \item Solving other form of problems with the algorithms that we have learned, as a means for real-world problem solutions.
\end{itemize}
